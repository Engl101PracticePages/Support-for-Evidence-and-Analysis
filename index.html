1) Mini lesson: what each sentence is supposed to do

In a research paragraph, you usually need all three:
  • Claim (C): your point (what you are saying)
  • Evidence (E): information from a source (quote or paraphrase + citation)
  • Analysis (A): your explanation (what the evidence means, why it matters, how it connects)
Common problem: students write C + E, and then their “analysis” sentence just repeats the evidence. If your sentence could start with “This shows…” and nothing changes, it might be repetition.

Quick self-test

If you delete your analysis sentence and your paragraph still “makes the same point,” your analysis probably isn’t doing enough work.

2) Practice: label sentences (C / E / A)

Directions: Read the short paragraph below. For each sentence, label it: C claim • E evidence • A analysis
Answer key:
  1. C — claim about the career (stable but demanding).
  2. E — info from a source + citation placeholder.
  3. A — explains what that evidence suggests about the job (meaning + implication).
  4. A — adds a tradeoff/complication, still analysis.

3) Analysis verb bank (use these instead of “This shows”)

Verbs that add meaning

  • suggests = points toward an idea (not 100% proof)
  • indicates = gives a sign that something is true
  • implies = hints at a conclusion
  • highlights = draws attention to what matters
  • illustrates = gives a clear example of

Verbs that go deeper (best for analysis)

  • complicates = adds a tradeoff / makes it less simple
  • limits = shows what the evidence does NOT prove
  • connects = links evidence back to your claim
  • demonstrates = shows strongly (use carefully)
  • raises = brings up a question/concern

Pick a verb + use a stem

Tip: pick “complicates” or “limits” if you want to sound more academic fast.
