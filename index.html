Support for Evidence and Analysis

This page helps you figure out what each sentence in a research paragraph is doing. The goal is to move from claim and evidence into real analysis, which means explaining what the evidence means and why it matters.

The big move

Do not stop after the source. Make the connection for your reader.

Step 1

Mini lesson: what each sentence is doing

C

Claim

Your point. This is what you are trying to say about your topic.

E

Evidence

Information from a source. This can be a quote, paraphrase, or summary with citation.

A

Analysis

Your explanation. This tells the reader what the evidence means, why it matters, and how it connects.

Common issue: students often write C + E, then their “analysis” sentence just repeats the evidence. Analysis should add a new layer of meaning.

Quick test: If you delete your analysis sentence and the paragraph still makes the same point, the analysis probably is not doing enough work yet.

Step 2

Practice: label the sentences

Read the sample paragraph. Then label each sentence as C claim, E evidence, or A analysis.

Answer key

  1. C: This sentence makes a claim about the career. It says nursing can be stable and demanding.
  2. E: This sentence uses source information and a citation placeholder.
  3. A: This sentence explains what the evidence suggests about the skills needed for the job.
  4. A: This sentence adds a complication by connecting responsibility to possible stress.
Step 3

Analysis verb bank

These verbs help you move past “This shows.” They push you to explain, connect, or complicate the evidence.

Verbs that add meaning

  • suggestspoints toward an idea without claiming total proof
  • indicatesgives a sign that something may be true
  • impliespoints to an idea the source does not say directly
  • highlightsdraws attention to what matters
  • illustratesgives a clear example of a larger idea

Verbs that go deeper

  • complicatesadds a tradeoff or makes the point less simple
  • limitsshows what the evidence does not prove
  • connectslinks evidence back to your claim
  • demonstratesshows strongly, when the evidence is strong enough
  • raisesbrings up a question or concern
Choose a verb to see a sentence stem.

Tip: “complicates” and “limits” are helpful when your paragraph sounds too simple.

Try this with your own writing: Find a sentence in your draft where you explain evidence, or where you wrote something like “This shows.” Rewrite it so the sentence explains what the evidence means.

Step 4

Your paragraph

What to do: paste one paragraph from your draft, label what each sentence is doing, then write two improved analysis sentences.

You do not have to be perfect. The point is to notice whether your paragraph has enough analysis.

Your analysis should explain meaning and connection, not just restate facts.
